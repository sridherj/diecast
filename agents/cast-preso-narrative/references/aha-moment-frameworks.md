# Aha-Moment Frameworks Quick Reference

Compact patterns for designing narrative hooks and reveals. Source: aha-moment psychology research and playbook.

## 1. Self-Identification Hook
Open with a specific, recognizable pain scenario — not a feature description.
- "You spent 4 hours Monday doing X..."
- "You know someone who can help, but you can't remember who..."
- Viewer who's experienced the pain immediately projects themselves into the story.
- **Anti-pattern:** "Product X is a tool that..." (feature description, not pain acknowledgment)

## 2. Zeigarnik Effect (Tease Before Showing)
Unfinished tasks hold attention. Tease the result before revealing it.
- Show the setup, pause before the payoff. Let the audience wonder.
- Works in slides: hook slide sets up tension, reveal slide resolves it 1-3 slides later.
- Never resolve a hook on the same slide — the gap is what creates attention.

## 3. Contrast Principle (Old Way -> New Way)
Juxtapose inefficiency and elegance to make elegance self-evident.
- "On [old tool], doing X means: step 1, step 2, step 3, step 4. On [new approach]: one action."
- Keep it brief: 5-10 seconds of contrast, then move on. Don't belabor.
- The viewer doesn't need to be told it's better — the comparison does the work.

## 4. Multi-Moment Aha Progression
Complex products need multiple recognition moments. Structure as three progressive ahas:
1. **"Possible" aha:** "I can do X" — demonstrates basic capability
2. **"Unexpected" aha:** "It found something I didn't expect" — shows depth beyond the obvious
3. **"Actionable" aha:** "I can act on this right now" — connects insight to next step

Space these across the narrative arc. Don't cluster them. Each builds on the previous.

## 5. One Unmistakable Benefit Per Slide
Every slide should reinforce one clear message. If the audience can't state the slide's point in one sentence, the slide is doing too much.
- Test: "What does the audience gain from this slide?" If the answer is vague, split or sharpen.
- Information slides carry facts. Moment slides carry feelings. Don't mix.

## 6. The 10/50/30 Structure
For any presentation segment:
- **First 10%:** Name the pain. Be specific, not generic.
- **Middle 50%:** Show the solution in action. One clear demo/example.
- **Last 30%:** Show what changes — the outcome, the implication, the next step.

## Application Rules
- Every hook slide MUST have a corresponding reveal slide later in the flow
- Aha moments should map to L1 outcomes (presentation-level walk-aways)
- At least 30% of slides should be `information` type — all moments with no breathing room exhausts the audience
- Recommend presenting the strongest aha at ~60-70% through the deck (not the end)
